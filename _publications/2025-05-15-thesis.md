My Ph.D. dissertation argues that early-sepsis prediction in the ICU fails the clinician when it produces *only* a point estimate. The work develops three complementary contributions that together give a prediction the two properties a clinician needs: it has to be accurate enough to act on, and honest enough to decline when it shouldn't be trusted.

- **OnAI-Comp** (TCBB 2021): an online AI-experts-competing framework where a trusted selector picks, per patient, the best model out of a heterogeneous pool — with average-regret guarantees and LIME interpretability.
- **Sepsyn-OLCP** (arXiv:2503.14663): wraps that pipeline with split conformal prediction and re-casts selection as a *gap*-based online learner, so every prediction ships with a distribution-free confidence interval and the selector is robust to single-expert miscalibration.
- **NeuroSep-CP-LCB** (arXiv:2503.16708): replaces the expert pool with a neural contextual bandit that models a patient-specific reward function, and uses a Lower-Confidence-Bound bonus calibrated by conformal prediction to make offline policy learning safe.

The three pieces decompose cleanly — any one of them can be lifted and reused in other high-stakes sequential-decision domains where calibrated uncertainty is part of the deliverable.
